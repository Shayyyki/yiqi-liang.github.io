Attentiv Medical

Fall 2019 - present | New Venture Design, entrepreneurship@UBC


Team: Attentiv Medical (attentivmedical.com) is a medical device startup formed from the New Venture Design entrepreneurial capstone course, comprising of four students from UBC Engineering and two students from UBC Sauder School of Business. We are in early stages of protyping and customer discovery, and have been accepted to the e@UBC Lean Launchpad incubator and the Microsoft Startup Program.

About: A majority of hospital patients receive intravenous (IV) infusions, yet the failure rate is high. IV infiltration happens when IV fluids leak from the vein into tissues, causing tissue damage that require hospitalization, surgeries, and even amputation. We created Attentiv Medical to prevent patient complications from IV infiltration, with the goal of improving standards of care and reducing treatment expenditure. The Attentiv system uses machine learning to monitor bioelectric signals from inside the vein to detect early onset of infiltration, alerting the care team before permanent damage can occur.

Role: I worked closely with our clinical partners, include researchers, nurses, and hopsital managers around Vancouver to collect project requirements, with a focus on the neonatual ICU patient demographic. From literature research, I identified that bioelectric signals can be used for infiltration detection, and worked with other engineers to design and prototype a sensor-embedded catheter. I developed the support vector machine algorithm for blood-tissue discrimination and the statistical thresholding algorithm for infiltration detection, which we successfully tested in both animal carcass and phantom model trials. I participated in market sizing and financial modelling to support our business activities. I also directed and edited the video above!

Lession Learned: TLDR: biomedical development is HARD. Other teams in my cohort projected revenue after the first year, while we roadmapped our entry into the market more than five years into the future. Balancing innovation, safety, and user needs in our design required active engagement from our clinical, regulatory, and business advisors. It's true that startup founders wear many hats, and I'm happy that I got to experience the business side of building a company for the first time.

H4ptic Sensory Feedback for Prostheses

March 2019 - present | Hatching Health, Speak Out for Engineering


Team: Formed after a few hours of networking at the Hatching Health hackathon, the team consisted of five engineering students, a occupational therapy student, and a product design and cognitive science student. We continued the project with additional rounds of protyping and reached out to researchers in the community.

About: Modern myoelectric EMG prosthetics are becoming increasingly common, but despite the body’s naturally integrated sensory motor control loop which uses sensory feedback information to plan motor tasks, these prosthetics have seen very limited development in the sensory area. In fact, there doesn't exist a commercially available prosthetic arm with sensory feedback. To resolve this problem, we created H4ptic, a non-invasive device that captures tactile sensation from the prosthetic and transmits that sensation to the user’s arm. The scientific principle of the device is based on a theory called maladaptive cortical plasticity, and we seek to leverage this through implementing sensory substitution, a method which has been established in past literature. Our planned final design is a wireless glove with integrated touch and temperature sensors wireless connected to an armband with vibrotactile stimulation. The design can be adapted to people's existing prosthetics, making it very practical for people to integrate H4ptic into their daily lives.

Role: I led the literature review into somatosensory cortex remapping, phantom maps, neural plasticitiy and sensory substition to provide the project with a solid scientific foundation. I individually presented H4ptic's design at the IMechE's Speak Out for Engineering competition, winning first place at the Vancouver regionals and Americas semi-finals rounds. As a submission for the IDeA competition, I created the "draw my life" animation and edited the video above.

Lession Learned: The basis of this project was largely formulated on scientific research, which meant that the pains and gains we assigned to H4ptic were largely derived from secondary sources. If I had the bandwidth to continue this project is a fuller capacity, I would put emphasis towards collaborating with prosethic-using individuals to tailor the design to address challenges they face.

FraXure - Femur Fracture Treatment Project

September 2016 - present | UBC Biomedical Engineering Student Team (BEST)


Video by the UBC Communications & Marketing team.

Team: Fraxure (or the Femur Fracture Treatment Project) was founded as a project from UBC BEST's fact-finding trip to Kenyan and Ugandan. Since it's conception in 2015, FraXure has usually comprised of 8-12 UBC students from engineering, medicine, kinesiology, science, and business.

About: Trauma is a major cause of death and disability worldwide and motor vehicle accidents are the leading cause of nonfatal injuries, with femur fractures being the most common musculoskeletal injury. Effective femoral surgery is not easily accessible to patients in low income countries and as a result, patients are treated with other methods. One of the most common treatments is called skeletal traction, a non-surgical method of realigning bones through constant force application. It is a method that requires few resources and is not deemed surgical, but constrains a patient to a bed for 2-3 months and is often accompanied by complications including pin-tract infection, malunion, nonunion, blood clots, and pressure sores. FraXure aims to design a device that aligns and maintains alignment of a broken femur, while being compact and allowing more mobility than current methods. The project aims to design a device that will produce better patient outcomes, increase mobility, and decrease hospital cost and bed time compared to current traction treatment.The team is currently prototyping various designs of the device, while also conducting research on bone and fracture mechanics and bone healing. The team is working with a group of Orthopaedic Medicine students in Uganda to develop the device so it is context appropriate and will be successful in improving traction treatment in low income countries.

Role: I started on the team in a mechanical design role, but soon migrated to research and safety. The primary concern with our design is that any force applied over time can put the patient at risk for localized deep tissue injuries sustained from pressure-induced ischemia. I built a comprehensive literature review around understanding the relationship between pressure, time, and other contributing physiological factors. Based on undesired fracture treatment outcomes reported in clinical cases, I generated quantitative requirements for the engineering team to design with. I also partook in writing the testing guidelines for verifying that our device does not exceed allowable interface pressure.

Lession Learned: This project involved heavy consideration of the in-country socioeconomic context and local attitudes toward medical treatments. We aimed for a balance of optimizing cost and patient outcomes, while keeping materials accessible and minimizing changes to current practice. My research role of quantifying requirements for tissue interface pressure proved to be extremely difficult as I uncovered (over 2 years) that the research in the field has yielded many inconsistent numbers, without reaching consensus on the best practice regarding pressure ulcers. Ultimately, I had to make some tradeoffs between reaching utmost accuracy with actually making progress, but I am curious to know if any research will come out in the next years with answers I've been searching for...

MYOhome Gesture-Controlled Smarthome

January 2018 | nwHacks


Team: nwHacks is the biggest hackathon in Western Canada and mainly attracts a software-oriented demographic. My team, consisting of four mechanical engineers and a software developer, was one of the most hardware-focused teams.

About: Voice-controlled smarthome features isn't accessible for everyone, especially those with speech impediments or strong accents. Using the Myo Armband, users can control their smart home devices such as lights and fans at the flick of their wrist and arm. Based on how many devices there are, users can use the MyoHome web app to configure the gestures for each device, and save those customized gestures to a user profile. Although there are only 5 basic gestures built in to the Myo Connect app, there are essentially an infinite number of gestures that could be configured based on the raw data output from the accelerometer, EMG sensors, and gyroscope. See our Devpost page.

Role: I handled the design, layout, and physical construction of the smarthome prototype, working with very limited material (cardboard scrounged from hackathon freebie boxes). I also filmed and edited the video in the last hour of the hackathon!

Lession Learned: Hackathons always has an element of luck to them. Luckily, my team was able to conceptualize the idea, build the physical and hardware models, create a business case, and execute the demo in less than 24 hours. We heavily relied on our respective experiences to create MYOhome, but that unfortunately meant we couldn't explore and learn new things. Despite so, the project was lots of fun to do!

Thunderbird Cubesat

2014 - 2016 | UBC Orbit Satellite Design Team


Team: I was on the Structure subteam in my first year, then I moved to the Attitude Determination and Control Subteam (ADCS) the year after.

About: The Thunderbird Satellite's primary payload consists of two cameras intended for high-resolution imaging of Earth. The photos are evaluated for potential forest fire risks using on-board machine learning modules, trained on the ground, with capability to update these modules over the course of the mission. The use of two cameras enables multiple filters to be applied to the image sensor, allowing for infrared and visible light imaging.

Role: . I created designs for the satellite’s structural components on SolidWorks, including the solar panel deployment mechanism and a weight-saving structural chassis, which were prototyped using 3D printing and laser cutting. I wrote test algorithms in C and MATLAB for different stages of the orbit, such as de-tumbling, normal orbit, and biannual rotations, in order to keep the satellite on course and orientated correctly.
